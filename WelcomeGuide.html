Everyday things to know
💧 Drinking water

The tap water is fine for showers and dishes, but don't drink it. We supply a big jug of purified water in the kitchen, plus a clear pitcher for the fridge and a freezer "core" that snaps in to keep it cold. If you run out, just ask Ocean One staff for a refill.

🧻 Toilet paper

Roatán's septic system is sensitive — please throw all toilet paper into the waste bins rather than flushing. The cleaning staff empties the bins daily.

⚡ Electricity

Power blinks now and then on the island. Our complex has a generator, so outages are brief — but don't rely on the alarm clocks (they reset when power drops). Bonus: the alarm clocks have USB chargers built into the back.

❄ Air conditioning

Separate thermostats in each bedroom. At night, close the bedroom doors so the cool air doesn't mix with the warm third floor. Please keep villa doors closed when A/C is running, and turn units off if you're out for more than an hour.

★ The Island Essentials Exchange

There's a cabinet in the kitchen labeled "Island Essentials Exchange" — a little community closet where past guests have left extras for the next group. Sunscreen, pool toys, non-perishables, basic spices (salt, pepper, garlic powder are usually in the cabinet above the stove or here), and an umbrella. Help yourself to anything that makes your trip easier; leave behind anything you'd like to pass on.

For families — little things we tucked in

Stepstool under the sink in the queen/queen bedroom · Kids' dining set in the cabinet just left of the kitchen sink · Slippery patio when wet — roll out the blue rug from the corner · Tuck breakable decor up high if you've got curious little ones.

A small island-construction quirks tour

This is Roatán — a few small things are charmingly idiosyncratic. Here's what to know so day one doesn't have a "wait, how does this work?" moment. Ask the property manager at check-in to walk you through any of them.

A
Patio doors → the pool

Pull the right handle down — with the doors pushed tightly together.

The patio doors lock by pulling the right-side handle down while both doors are pressed together — the trick is pushing them together tightly so it catches. The left handle can be in any position. To check, try sliding the door — if it doesn't budge, you're good.

B
Upper deck door · Rooftop

Easier. Close, latch, push handle up — done.

When the rooftop deck door is closed and latched, just push the handle up and it returns to its normal position. The door is now locked. Worth the trip — the rooftop is one of our favorite spots on the island.

C
The TV

Ask the property manager at check-in to walk you through it.

Smart TV + cable box. Turn on both, then select HDMI 1, 2, or 3 to access channels and streaming. It's not complicated, but it's easier to learn the input dance once than to fuss with it at 9pm.

D
The safe

Instructions are taped to the top of the safe.

Follow the printed instructions. The important bit: to reset the code, open the battery cover inside the safe and press the small button. Then enter your new code.

E
Jacuzzi

Flip it on, give it 1–2 hours to warm up.

Pre-configured for you. When the two small red lines are visible, it's working properly. Please turn it off after use — sea breezes will steal the heat overnight.  Leave the cover on, otherwise it will not remain hot.

There are more restaurants than you'll ever need within walking distance. We also recommend a water taxi to West End to check out the places there. You really can't go wrong — and food & drinks here are cheaper than most other islands we've traveled to.

Currency · Conversion

The US Dollar works everywhere.

No need to stress about exchanging money before you arrive. Occasionally prices are listed in Honduran Lempiras, but vendors still take USD.

Heads-up: if your dollar bill has even a small tear, it may be rejected — bring crisp bills if you can.

The rate, roughly
26L ≈ $1 USD

So something priced at 100L is about $4. Or open the iPhone Calculator → tap the icon → toggle Convert.

Credit cards

Bring Visa or Mastercard.

Accepted most places, but not all. Amex is hit-or-miss — carry a backup. Vendors rarely charge a card fee, and paying in Lempiras usually gives you a better rate than the vendor would offer. Use a card with no foreign-transaction fees, even paying in USD.

Tipping tip

Tell the server before they swipe.

Paying by card at restaurants, you usually need to tell your server the tip amount before they run the card — there's often no line to write it in. If you forget, cash tips are always welcome. Rule of thumb: ~15%.

Small bills

Your best friend in Roatán.

We always bring a stash of $1s and $5s — a few hundred dollars' worth — and it makes life so much easier. Local vendors and service staff really appreciate small bills for tips, snacks, taxis, and quick purchases.

Sales tax surprise

15%–19%, depending.

Sales tax in Roatán is steep. If a price doesn't include tax it can be a surprise at checkout — most vendors will tell you whether tax is baked in, but when in doubt, just ask. No one likes unexpected math on vacation.

★ The main event

Some of the best snorkeling in the world — just down the beach.

Walk out to the beach, turn left, and head all the way to the end where the rocks are. Enter the water and swim straight out to the reef, then go right. There's a huge reef around the island, and a big shelf with a drop-off. You can snorkel here for hours.

For little kids

Swimming with the fishies.

In the shallow water by the rocks, you can stand and be surrounded by colorful fish. For families with little ones, this is always the highlight — now anywhere we travel, our kids are disappointed when they're not surrounded by fish in shallow water.

Can't-miss

Scuba diving.

If you dive, you can't come to Roatán without diving. Extremely affordable; the reef is close so boat rides are short. Night dives, shipwrecks, drift dives — you name it. Favorite shop: West Bay Divers. Ocean One also offers guest dive discounts at the private dive center — call +504 9450-5014.

Day trip · weather-permitting

Cayos Cochinos.

Tiny remote islands about two hours away with some of the most pristine, untouched snorkeling we've ever seen. Most tours include a fresh fish lunch cooked for you by the locals. Not cheap — definitely worth it. Boats only go if the weather is good.

Boats · booze · snorkel

Mono Loco.

Snorkel tours, sunset tours, dinner cruises. The food and drinks are fantastic and the trips are super fun — music, dancing, partying. Kids loved it too.
